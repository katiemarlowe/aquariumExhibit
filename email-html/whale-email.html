The North Atlantic right whale is one of the most endangered whales in the world. Its name comes from the idea that it was the "right" whale to hunt: it was slow moving, floated after death, and had enormous amounts of oil and baleen. Even though commercial whaling ended in 1935, fewer than 500 right whales are alive today. You won't see any right whales in the Aquarium, but you may spy one on an Aquarium Whale Watch.

During feeding season, which is typically from Spring until Fall, each whale eats more than 2,600 pounds of zooplankton a day. They eat about 4% of their body weight a day, which would be the equivalent of a 150 pound person eating 6 pounds of food. Since they can’t use all of that energy, they store the excess as blubber.

Right Whales, like other whales, dolphins, seals, and sea lions, are mammals, and give birth to a single calf every 3-5 years. The calves are 13-15 feet long at birth, and their baleen starts out soft, but they grow quickly and their baleen soon stiffens.
